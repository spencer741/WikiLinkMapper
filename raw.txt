srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Split_Aloe.jpg/450px-Split_Aloe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Split_Aloe.jpg/600px-Split_Aloe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1512" data-file-height="2016" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Split_Aloe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Succulent plants store water in their fleshy stems, or leaves, such as this <i><a href="/wiki/Aloe" title="Aloe">Aloe</a></i></div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>, <b>succulent plants</b>, also known as <b>succulents</b>, are <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> that have some parts that are more than normally thickened and fleshy, usually to retain water in <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> climates or <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> conditions. The word "succulent" comes from the Latin word <i>sucus</i>, meaning juice, or sap.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Succulent plants may store water in various structures, such as <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stems</a>. Some definitions also include <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a>, thus <a href="/wiki/Geophyte" class="mw-redirect" title="Geophyte">geophytes</a> that survive unfavorable periods by dying back to underground <a href="/wiki/Storage_organ" title="Storage organ">storage organs</a> may be regarded as succulents. In <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticultural</a> use, the term "succulent" is sometimes used in a way which excludes plants that <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botanists</a> would regard as succulents, such as <a href="/wiki/Cactus" title="Cactus">cacti</a>. Succulents are often grown as <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plants</a> because of their striking and unusual appearance.\n</p><p>Many plant families have multiple succulents found within them (over 25 plant families).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In some families, such as <a href="/wiki/Aizoaceae" title="Aizoaceae">Aizoaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cactaceae" class="mw-redirect" title="Cactaceae">Cactaceae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crassulaceae" title="Crassulaceae">Crassulaceae</a>, most species are succulents. The habitats of these water preserving plants are often in areas with high temperatures and low rainfall. Succulents have the ability to thrive on limited water sources, such as mist and dew, which makes them equipped to survive in an ecosystem which contains scarce water sources.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Succulent_plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Succulent_in_San_Francisco.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Succulent_in_San_Francisco.JPG/220px-Succulent_in_San_Francisco.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Succulent_in_San_Francisco.JPG/330px-Succulent_in_San_Francisco.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Succulent_in_San_Francisco.JPG/440px-Succulent_in_San_Francisco.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Succulent_in_San_Francisco.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Center of a succulent (<i><a href="/wiki/Aloe_polyphylla" title="Aloe polyphylla">Aloe polyphylla</a></i>)</div></div></div>\n<p>A general definition of succulents is that they are <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> resistant plants in which the leaves, stem or roots have become more than usually fleshy by the development of water-storing tissue.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERowley19801_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERowley19801-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Other sources exclude roots as in the definition "a plant with thick, fleshy and swollen stems and/or leaves, adapted to dry environments."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeentje2010116_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeentje2010116-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This difference affects the relationship between succulents and "geophytes" \xe2\x80\x93 plants that survive unfavorable seasons as a resting bud on an underground organ.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeentje201032_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeentje201032-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> These underground organs, such as bulbs, corms and tubers, are often fleshy with water-storing tissues. Thus if roots are included in the definition, many geophytes would be classed as succulents. Plants adapted to living in dry environments such as succulents are termed <i><a href="/wiki/Xerophyte" title="Xerophyte">xerophytes</a></i>. However, not all xerophytes are succulents, since there are other ways of adapting to a shortage of water, e.g., by developing small leaves which may roll up or having leathery rather than succulent leaves.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Nor are all succulents xerophytes, since plants like <i><a href="/wiki/Crassula_helmsii" title="Crassula helmsii">Crassula helmsii</a></i> are both succulent and aquatic.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Those who grow succulents as a hobby use the term in a different way to botanists. In horticultural use, the term <i>succulent</i> regularly excludes cacti. For example, Jacobsen\'s three volume <i>Handbook of Succulent Plants</i> does not cover cacti,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacobsen1960_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacobsen1960-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and "cacti and succulents" is the title or part of the title of many books covering the cultivation of these plants.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson1999_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson1999-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHecht1994_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHecht1994-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewitt1993_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewitt1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, in botanical terminology, cacti are succulents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERowley19801_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERowley19801-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Horticulturists may also exclude other groups of plants, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Bromeliads" class="mw-redirect" title="Bromeliads">bromeliads</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInnesWall1995_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInnesWall1995-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A practical, but unscientific, horticultural definition is "a succulent plant is any desert plant that a succulent plant collector wishes to grow."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartinChapman1977_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartinChapman1977-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Such plants less often include geophytes (in which the swollen storage organ is wholly underground) but do include plants with a <a href="/wiki/Caudex" title="Caudex">caudex</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartinChapman197719-20_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartinChapman197719-20-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> which is a swollen above-ground organ at soil level, formed from a stem, a root or both.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeentje201032_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeentje201032-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A further difficulty is that plants are not either <i>succulent</i> or <i>non-succulent</i>. In many genera and families there is a continuous gradation from plants with thin leaves and normal stems to those with very clearly thickened and fleshy leaves or stems, so that deciding what is a succulent is often arbitrary. Different sources may classify the same species differently.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERowley19802_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERowley19802-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Succulent_plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eze_jardin_exotique.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Eze_jardin_exotique.JPG/220px-Eze_jardin_exotique.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Eze_jardin_exotique.JPG/330px-Eze_jardin_exotique.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Eze_jardin_exotique.JPG/440px-Eze_jardin_exotique.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eze_jardin_exotique.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A collection of succulent plants, including cacti, from the <a href="/wiki/Jardin_botanique_d%27%C3%88ze" title="Jardin botanique d&#39;\xc3\x88ze">Jardin botanique d\'\xc3\x88ze</a>, France</div></div></div>\n<p>The storage of water often gives succulent plants a more swollen or fleshy appearance than other plants, a characteristic known as succulence. In addition to succulence, succulent plants variously have other water-saving features. These may include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Crassulacean_acid_metabolism" title="Crassulacean acid metabolism">Crassulacean acid metabolism</a> (CAM) to minimize water loss</li>\n<li>absent, reduced, or cylindrical-to-spherical leaves</li>\n<li>reduction in the number of <a href="/wiki/Stoma" title="Stoma">stomata</a></li>\n<li>stems as the main site of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, rather than leaves</li>\n<li>compact, reduced, cushion-like, columnar, or spherical growth form</li>\n<li>ribs enabling rapid increases in plant volume and decreasing surface area exposed to the sun</li>\n<li>waxy, hairy, or spiny outer surface to create a humid micro-habitat around the plant, which reduces air movement near the surface of the plant, and thereby reduces water loss and creates shade</li>\n<li>roots very near the surface of the soil, so they are able to take up moisture from very small showers or even from heavy <a href="/wiki/Dew" title="Dew">dew</a></li>\n<li>ability to remain plump and full of water even with high internal temperatures (e.g., 52&#160;\xc2\xb0C or 126&#160;\xc2\xb0F)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEComptonn.d._16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEComptonn.d.-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>very impervious outer <a href="/wiki/Plant_cuticle" title="Plant cuticle">cuticle</a> (skin)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEComptonn.d._16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEComptonn.d.-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilaginous substances</a>, which retain water abundantly<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEComptonn.d._16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEComptonn.d.-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span High temperatures and low <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> force plants to collect and store water to survive long dry periods. Some species of <a href="/wiki/Cactus" title="Cactus">cactus</a> can survive for months without rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Succulents may occasionally occur as <a href="/wiki/Epiphyte" title="Epiphyte">epiphytes</a>, growing on other plants with limited or no contact with the ground, and are dependent on their ability to store water and gain nutrients by other means; this niche is seen in <i><a href="/wiki/Tillandsia" title="Tillandsia">Tillandsia</a></i>. Succulents also occur as inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a> <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coasts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dry_lake" title="Dry lake">dry lakes</a>, which are title="Crassula">Crassula</a></i>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Succulents are favored as houseplants for their attractiveness and ease of care. If properly potted, succulents require little maintenance to survive indoors.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Succulents are very adaptable <a href="/wiki/Houseplant" title="Houseplant">houseplants</a> and will thrive in a range of indoor conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  For most plant owners, over-watering and associated infections are the main cause of death in succulents.\n</p><p>Succulents can be propagated by different means. The most common is vegetative propagation; this includes <i>cuttings</i> where several inches of stem with leaves are cut and after healing produce a callus. After a week or so, roots may grow. A second method is <i>division</i> consisting of uprooting an overgrown clump and pulling the stems and roots apart. The easiest one is allowing the formation of callus from a leaf.
