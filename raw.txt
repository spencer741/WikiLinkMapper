title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">Organisms</a> of many species are specialized into <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> and <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female</a> varieties, each known as a <b>sex</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stevenson_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Purves_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purves-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">Sexual reproduction</a> involves the combining and mixing of <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> traits: specialized <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gametes</a> combine to form offspring that inherit traits from each parent. The gametes produced by an organism define its sex: males produce small gametes (e.g. spermatozoa, or <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a>, in animals; <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spermatophyte" title="Spermatophyte">seed plants</a>) while females produce large gametes (<a href="/wiki/Ovum" class="mw-redirect" title="Ovum">ova</a>, or egg cells). Individual organisms which produce both male and female gametes are termed <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite" title="Hermaphrodite">hermaphroditic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Purves_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purves-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Gametes can be identical in form and function (known as <a href="/wiki/Isogamy" title="Isogamy">isogamy</a>), but, in many cases, an asymmetry has evolved such that two different types of gametes (heterogametes) exist (known as <a href="/wiki/Anisogamy" title="Anisogamy">anisogamy</a>).\n</p><p>Physical differences are often associated with the different sexes of an organism; these <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphisms</a> can reflect the different reproductive pressures the sexes experience. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Mate_choice" title="Mate choice">mate choice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_selection" title="Sexual selection">sexual selection</a> can accelerate the evolution of physical differences between the sexes.\n</p><p>Among humans and other <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, males typically carry an X and a <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a> (XY), whereas females typically carry two <a href="/wiki/X_chromosome" title="X chromosome">X chromosomes</a> (XX), which are a part of the <a href="/wiki/XY_sex-determination_system" title="XY sex-determination system">XY sex-determination system</a>. Humans may also be <a href="/wiki/Intersex" title="Intersex">intersex</a>. Other animals have different <a href="/wiki/Sex-determination_system" title="Sex-determination system">sex-determination systems</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/ZW_sex-determination_system" title="ZW sex-determination system">ZW system</a> in birds, the <a href="/wiki/X0_sex-determination_system" title="X0 sex-determination system">X0 system</a> in insects, and various <a href="/wiki/Environmental_sex_determination" title="Environmental sex determination">environmental systems</a>, for example in crustaceans. <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">Fungi</a> may also have more complex <a href="/wiki/Allelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Allelic">allelic</a> mating systems, with sexes not accurately described as male, female, or hermaphroditic.<sup id="cite_ref-Watkinson_Boddy_Money_2015_p._115_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watkinson_Boddy_Money_2015_p._115-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" title="Ovum">ovum</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p>One of the basic properties of life is reproduction, the capacity to generate new individuals, and sex is an aspect of this process. Life has evolved from simple stages to more complex ones, and so have the reproduction mechanisms. Initially the reproduction was a replicating process that consists in producing new individuals that contain the same genetic information as the original or parent individual. This mode of reproduction is called <i>asexual</i>, and it is still used by many species, particularly unicellular, but it is also very common in multicellular organisms, including many of those with sexual reproduction.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In sexual reproduction, the genetic material of the offspring comes from two different individuals. As sexual reproduction developed by way of a long process of evolution, intermediates exist. Bacteria, for instance, reproduce asexually, but undergo a process by which a part of the genetic material of an individual donor is transferred to another recipient.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Disregarding intermediates, the basic distinction between asexual and sexual reproduction is the way in which the genetic material is processed. Typically, prior to an asexual division, a cell duplicates its genetic information content, and then divides. This process of cell division is called <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a>. In sexual reproduction, there are special kinds of cells that divide without prior duplication of its genetic material, in a process named <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>. The resulting cells are called <a href="/wiki/Gametes" class="mw-redirect" title="Gametes">gametes</a>, and contain only half the genetic material of the parent cells. These gametes are the cells that are prepared for the sexual reproduction of the organism.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Sex comprises the arrangements that enable sexual reproduction, and has evolved alongside the reproduction system, starting with similar gametes (isogamy) and progressing to systems that have different gamete types, such as those involving a large female gamete (ovum) and a small male gamete (sperm).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In complex organisms, the <a href="/wiki/Sex_organ" title="Sex organ">sex organs</a> are the parts that are involved in the production and exchange of gametes in sexual reproduction. Many species, both plants and animals, have sexual specialization, and their populations are divided into male and female individuals. Conversely, there are also species in which there is no sexual specialization, and the same individuals both contain masculine and feminine reproductive organs, and they are called <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodites" class="mw-redirect" title="Hermaphrodites">hermaphrodites</a>. This is very frequent in plants.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span></h2>\n<div single-celled <a href="/wiki/Eukaryotes" class="mw-redirect" title="Eukaryotes">eukaryotes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The reason for the evolution of sex, and the reason(s) it has survived to the present, are still matters of debate. Some of the many plausible theories include: that sex creates variation among offspring, sex helps in the spread of advantageous traits, that sex helps in the removal of disadvantageous traits, and that sex facilitates repair of germ-line DNA.\n</p><p>Sexual reproduction is a process specific to <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a>, organisms whose cells contain a nucleus and mitochondria. In addition to animals, plants, and fungi, <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">other eukaryotes</a> (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> parasite) also engage in sexual reproduction. Some bacteria use <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_conjugation" title="Bacterial conjugation">conjugation</a> to transfer genetic material between cells; while not the same as sexual reproduction, this also results in the mixture of genetic traits.\n</p><p>The defining characteristic of sexual reproduction in eukaryotes is the difference between the gametes and the binary nature of fertilization. Multiplicity of gamete types within a species would still be considered a form of sexual reproduction. However, no third gamete type is known in multicellular plants or animals.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While the evolution of sex dates to the prokaryote or early eukaryote stage,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> the origin of <a href="/wiki/Sex-determination_system" title="Sex-determination system">chromosomal sex determination</a> may have been fairly early in eukaryotes (see <a href="/wiki/Anisogamy#Evolution_of_anisogamy" title="Anisogamy">evolution of anisogamy</a>). The <a href="/wiki/ZW_sex-determination_system" title="ZW sex-determination system">ZW sex-determination system</a> is shared by birds, some fish and some <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>. XY sex determination is used by most mammals,<sup id="cite_ref-pmid18581056_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18581056-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> but also some insects,<sup id="cite_ref-pmid21047257_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21047257-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and plants (<i>Silene latifolia</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-pmid9607762_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9607762-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/X0_sex-determination_system" title="X0 sex-determination system">X0 sex-determination</a> is found in most <a href="/wiki/Arachnid" title="Arachnid">arachnids</a>, insects such as <a href="/wiki/Silverfish" title="Silverfish">silverfish</a> (<a href="/wiki/Apterygota" title="Apterygota">Apterygota</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a> (<a href="/wiki/Paleoptera" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleoptera">Paleoptera</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a> (<a href="/wiki/Exopterygota" title="Exopterygota">Exopterygota</a>), and some nematodes, crustaceans, and gastropods.<sup id="cite_ref-Evolution_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evolution-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>No genes are shared between the avian ZW and mammal XY chromosomes,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and from a comparison between chicken and human, the Z chromosome appeared similar to the <a href="/wiki/Autosomal" class="mw-redirect" title="Autosomal">autosomal</a> chromosome 9 in human, rather than X or Y, suggesting that the ZW and XY sex-determination systems do not share an origin, but that the sex chromosomes are derived from autosomal chromosomes of the <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">common ancestor</a> of birds and mammals.\nA paper from 2004 compared the chicken Z chromosome with <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">platypus</a> X chromosomes and suggested that the two systems are related.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_reproduction">Sexual reproduction</span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">Sexual reproduction</a></div> <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Isogamy" title="Isogamy">Isogamy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anisogamy" title="Anisogamy">Anisogamy</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sexual_cycle.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Sexual_cycle.svg/220px-Sexual_cycle.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Sexual_cycle.svg/330px-Sexual_cycle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Sexual_cycle.svg/440px-Sexual_cycle.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sexual_cycle.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The life cycle of sexually reproducing organisms cycles through haploid and diploid stages</div></div></div>\n<p>Sexual reproduction in eukaryotes is a process whereby organisms form offspring that combine genetic traits from both parents. Chromosomes are passed on from one generation to the next in this process. Each cell in the offspring has half the chromosomes of the mother and half of the father.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\nGenetic traits are contained within the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">deoxyribonucleic acid</a> (DNA) of <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosomes</a>\xe2\x80\x94by combining one of each type of chromosomes from each parent, an organism is formed containing a doubled set of chromosomes. This double-chromosome stage is called "<a href="/wiki/Diploid" class="mw-redirect" title="Diploid">diploid</a>", while the single-chromosome stage is "<a href="/wiki/Haploid" class="mw-redirect" title="Haploid">haploid</a>". Diploid organisms can, in turn, form haploid cells (<a href="/wiki/Gametes" class="mw-redirect" title="Gametes">gametes</a>) that randomly contain one of each of the chromosome pairs, via <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Meiosis also involves a stage of <a href="/wiki/Chromosomal_crossover" title="Chromosomal crossover">chromosomal crossover</a>, in which regions of DNA are exchanged between matched types of chromosomes, to form a new pair of mixed chromosomes. <a href="/wiki/Chromosomal_crossover" title="Chromosomal crossover">Crossing over</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a> (the recombining of single sets of chromosomes to make a new diploid) result in the new organism containing a different set of genetic traits from either parent.\n</p><p>In many organisms, the haploid stage has been reduced to just <a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gametes</a> specialized to recombine and form a new diploid organism; in others, such as <a href="/wiki/Cryptogam" title="Cryptogam">cryptogamic plants</a> the gametes are capable of undergoing <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">cell division</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Multicellular" class="mw-redirect" title="Multicellular">multicellular</a> haploid organisms. In either case, gametes may be externally similar, particularly in size (<a href="/wiki/Isogamy" title="Isogamy">isogamy</a>), or may have <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> an asymmetry such that the gametes are different in size and other aspects (<a href="/wiki/Anisogamy" title="Anisogamy">anisogamy</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\nBy convention, the larger gamete (called an <a href="/wiki/Ovum" class="mw-redirect" title="Ovum">ovum</a>, or egg cell) is considered female, while the smaller gamete (called a spermatozoon, or sperm cell) is considered male. An individual that produces exclusively large gametes is female, and one that produces exclusively small gametes is male.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> An individual that produces both types of gametes is a <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite" title="Hermaphrodite">hermaphrodite</a>; in some cases hermaphrodites are able to <a href="/wiki/Self-fertilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-fertilization">self-fertilize</a> and produce offspring on their own, without a second organism.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction_in_animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexual reproduction in animals">Sexual reproduction in animals</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hoverflies_mating_midair.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Hoverflies_mating_midair.jpg/220px-Hoverflies_mating_midair.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Hoverflies_mating_midair.jpg/330px-Hoverflies_mating_midair.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Hoverflies_mating_midair.jpg/440px-Hoverflies_mating_midair.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1067" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hoverflies_mating_midair.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hoverfly" title="Hoverfly">Hoverflies</a> mating</div></div></div>\n<p>Most sexually reproducing animals spend their lives as diploid, with the haploid stage reduced to single-cell gametes.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The gametes of animals have male and female forms\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Spermatozoa" class="mw-redirect" title="Spermatozoa">spermatozoa</a> and egg cells. These gametes combine to form embryos which develop into a new organism.\n</p><p>The male gamete, a <a href="/wiki/Spermatozoon" title="Spermatozoon">spermatozoon</a> (produced in vertebrates within the <a href="/wiki/Testes" class="mw-redirect" title="Testes">testes</a>), is a small cell containing a single long <a href="/wiki/Flagellum" title="Flagellum">flagellum</a> which propels it.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\nSpermatozoa are extremely reduced cells, lacking many cellular components that would be necessary for embryonic development. They are specialized for motility, seeking out an egg cell and fusing with it in a process called <a href="/wiki/Fertilization#Fertilisation_in_animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a>.\n</p><p>Female gametes are egg cells (produced in vertebrates within the <a href="/wiki/Ovary" title="Ovary">ovaries</a>), large immobile cells that contain the nutrients and cellular components necessary for a developing embryo.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\nEgg cells are often associated with other cells which support the development of the embryo, forming an <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">egg</a>. In mammals, the fertilized embryo instead develops within the female, receiving nutrition directly from its mother.\n</p><p>Animals are usually mobile and seek out a partner of the opposite sex for <a href="/wiki/Mating" title="Mating">mating</a>. Animals which live in the water can mate using <a href="/wiki/External_fertilization" title="External fertilization">external fertilization</a>, where the eggs and sperm are released into and combine within the surrounding water.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Most animals that live outside of water, however, use <a href="/wiki/Internal_fertilization" title="Internal fertilization">internal fertilization</a>, transferring sperm directly from to female to prevent the gametes from drying up.\n</p><p>In most birds, both excretion and reproduction is done through a single posterior opening, called the <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloaca</a>\xe2\x80\x94male and female birds touch cloaca to transfer sperm, a process called "cloacal kissing".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In many other terrestrial animals, males use specialized sex organs to assist the transport of sperm\xe2\x80\x94these <a href="/wiki/Male_sex_organs" class="mw-redirect" title="Male sex organs">male sex organs</a> are called <a href="/wiki/Intromittent_organ" title="Intromittent organ">intromittent organs</a>. In humans and other mammals this male organ is the <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">penis</a>, which enters the female reproductive tract (called the <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vagina</a>) to achieve <a href="/wiki/Insemination" title="Insemination">insemination</a>\xe2\x80\x94a process called <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a>. The penis contains a tube through which <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a> (a fluid containing sperm) travels. In female mammals the vagina connects with the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a>, an organ which directly supports the development of a fertilized embryo within (a process called <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">gestation</a>).\n</p><p>Because of their motility, <a href="/wiki/Animal_sexual_behavior" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal sexual behavior">animal sexual behavior</a> can involve coercive sex. <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_insemination" title="Traumatic insemination">Traumatic insemination</a>, for example, is used by some insect species to inseminate females through a wound in the abdominal cavity\xe2\x80\x94a process detrimental to the female\'s health.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plants">Plants</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mature_flower_diagram.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Mature_flower_diagram.svg/300px-Mature_flower_diagram.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Mature_flower_diagram.svg/450px-Mature_flower_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Mature_flower_diagram.svg/600px-Mature_flower_diagram.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="693" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mature_flower_diagram.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flowers are the sexual organs of flowering plants, usually containing both male and female parts.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plant_reproduction" title="Plant reproduction">Plant reproduction</a></div>\n<p>Like animals, plants have developed specialized male and female gametes.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Within seed plants, male gametes are contained within hard coats, forming <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>. The female gametes of seed plants are contained within <a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">ovules</a>; once fertilized by pollen these form <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> which, like eggs, contain the nutrients necessary for the development of the embryonic plant.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright" style="background:#f9f9f9; border:1px solid #ccc; margin:0.5em;">\n<table border="0" cellpadding="2" cellspacing="0" style="width:260px; font-size:85%; border:1px solid #ccc; margin:0.3em;">\n\n<tbody><tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/File:Pinus_nigra_cone.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Pinus nigra cone.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Pinus_nigra_cone.jpg/152px-Pinus_nigra_cone.jpg" decoding="async" width="152" height="180" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Pinus_nigra_cone.jpg/228px-Pinus_nigra_cone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Pinus_nigra_cone.jpg/303px-Pinus_nigra_cone.jpg 2x" data-file-width="612" data-file-height="726" /></a></td>\n<td><a href="/wiki/File:Pine_cones,_immature_male.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Pine cones, immature male.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Pine_cones%2C_immature_male.jpg/104px-Pine_cones%2C_immature_male.jpg" decoding="async" width="104" height="180" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Pine_cones%2C_immature_male.jpg/156px-Pine_cones%2C_immature_male.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Pine_cones%2C_immature_male.jpg/208px-Pine_cones%2C_immature_male.jpg 2x" data-file-width="661" data-file-height="1144" /></a>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<div style="border: none; width:260px;" class="thumbcaption">Female (left) and male (right) cones are the sex organs of pines and other conifers.</div></div>\n<p>Many plants have <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> and these are the sexual organs of those plants. Flowers are usually hermaphroditic, producing both male and female gametes. The female parts, in the center of a flower, are the <a href="/wiki/Pistils" class="mw-redirect" title="Pistils">pistils</a>, each unit consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Carpel" class="mw-redirect" title="Carpel">carpel</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Style_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Style (botany)">style</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Stigma_(botany)" title="Stigma (botany)">stigma</a>. One or more of these reproductive units may be merged to form a single compound <a href="/wiki/Pistil" class="mw-redirect" title="Pistil">pistil</a>. Within the carpels are <a href="/wiki/Ovules" class="mw-redirect" title="Ovules">ovules</a> which develop into seeds after fertilization. The male parts of the flower are the <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a>: these consist of long filaments arranged between the pistil and the petals that produce pollen in <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_botanical_terms#anther" title="Glossary of botanical terms">anthers</a> at their tips. When a pollen grain lands upon the stigma on top of a carpel\'s style, it germinates to produce a <a href="/wiki/Pollen_tube" title="Pollen tube">pollen tube</a> that grows down through the tissues of the style into the carpel, where it delivers male gamete nuclei to fertilize an ovule that eventually develops into a seed.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifers</a> the sex organs are <a href="/wiki/Conifer_cone" title="Conifer cone">conifer cones</a> and have male and female forms. The more familiar female cones are typically more durable, containing ovules within them. Male cones are smaller and produce pollen which is transported by wind to land in female cones. As with flowers, seeds form within the female cone after pollination.\n</p><p>Because plants are immobile, they depend upon passive methods for transporting pollen grains to other plants. Many plants, including conifers and grasses, produce lightweight pollen which is carried by wind to neighboring plants. Other plants have heavier, sticky pollen that is specialized for transportation by insects. The plants attract these insects or larger animals such as <a href="/wiki/Humming_birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Humming birds">humming birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bats" class="mw-redirect" title="Bats">bats</a> with nectar-containing flowers. These animals transport the pollen as they move to other flowers, which also contain female reproductive organs, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fungi">Fungi</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mating_in_fungi" title="Mating in fungi">Mating in fungi</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shiitake_mushroom.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Shiitake_mushroom.jpg/220px-Shiitake_mushroom.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Shiitake_mushroom.jpg/330px-Shiitake_mushroom.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Shiitake_mushroom.jpg/440px-Shiitake_mushroom.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2700" data-file-height="1816" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shiitake_mushroom.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mushrooms are produced as part of fungal sexual reproduction</div></div></div>\n<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> reproduce sexually, having both a haploid and diploid stage in their life cycles. These fungi are typically <a href="/wiki/Isogamy" title="Isogamy">isogamous</a>, lacking male and female specialization: haploid fungi grow into contact with each other and then fuse their cells. In some of these cases, the fusion is asymmetric, and the cell which donates only a nucleus (and not accompanying cellular material) could arguably be considered "male".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Fungi may also have more complex allelic mating systems, with other sexes not accurately described as male, female, or hermaphroditic.<sup id="cite_ref-Watkinson_Boddy_Money_2015_p._115_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watkinson_Boddy_Money_2015_p._115-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some fungi, including <a href="/wiki/Baker%27s_yeast" title="Baker&#39;s yeast">baker\'s yeast</a>, have <a href="/wiki/Mating_type" title="Mating type">mating types</a> that create a duality similar to male and female roles. Yeast with the same mating type will not fuse with each other to form diploid cells, only with yeast carrying the other mating type.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many species of <a href="/wiki/Dikarya" title="Dikarya">higher fungi</a> produce <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">mushrooms</a> as part of their <a href="/wiki/Fungus#Reproduction" title="Fungus">sexual reproduction</a>. Within the mushroom diploid cells are formed, later dividing into haploid <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spores</a>. The height of the mushroom aids the <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">dispersal</a> of these sexually produced offspring.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sex_determination">Sex determination</span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sex-determination_system" title="Sex-determination system">Sex-determination system</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Evolsex-dia2a.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Evolsex-dia2a.svg/300px-Evolsex-dia2a.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Evolsex-dia2a.svg/450px-Evolsex-dia2a.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Evolsex-dia2a.svg/600px-Evolsex-dia2a.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="483" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Evolsex-dia2a.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sex helps the spread of advantageous traits through recombination. The diagrams compare evolution of allele frequency in a sexual population (top) and an asexual population (bottom). The vertical axis shows frequency and the horizontal axis shows time. The alleles a/A and b/B occur at random. The advantageous alleles A and B, arising independently, can be rapidly combined by sexual reproduction into the most advantageous combination AB. Asexual reproduction takes longer to achieve this combination, because it can only produce AB if A arises in an individual which already has B, or vice versa.</div></div></div>\n<p>The most basic sexual system is one in which all organisms are <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite" title="Hermaphrodite">hermaphrodites</a>, producing both male and female gametes\xe2\x80\x94<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> this is true of some animals (e.g. snails) and the majority of flowering plants.<sup id="cite_ref-dellaporta_1993_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dellaporta_1993-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In many cases, however, specialization of sex has evolved such that some organisms produce only male or only female gametes. The biological cause for an organism developing into one sex or the other is called <i>sex determination</i>.  The cause may be genetic or non-genetic.  Within animals and other organisms that have <b>genetic sex</b> systems, the determining factor may be the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Sex_chromosome" title="Sex chromosome">sex chromosome</a>, or it may be other genetic differences.  Non-genetic systems use environmental cues, such as the temperature during early development, to determine the sex of the offspring.\n</p><p>In the majority of species with sex specialization, organisms are either male (producing only male gametes) or female (producing only female gametes). Exceptions are common\xe2\x80\x94for example, the roundworm <i><a href="/wiki/Caenorhabditis_elegans" title="Caenorhabditis elegans">C. elegans</a></i> has an hermaphrodite and a male sex (a system called <a href="/wiki/Androdioecy" title="Androdioecy">androdioecy</a>).\n</p><p>Sometimes an organism\'s development is intermediate between male and female, a condition called <a href="/wiki/Intersex" title="Intersex">intersex</a>. Sometimes intersex individuals are called "hermaphrodite"; but, unlike biological hermaphrodites, intersex individuals are unusual cases and are not typically fertile in both male and female aspects.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic">Genetic</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Drosophila_XY_sex-determination.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Drosophila_XY_sex-determination.svg/220px-Drosophila_XY_sex-determination.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Drosophila_XY_sex-determination.svg/330px-Drosophila_XY_sex-determination.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Drosophila_XY_sex-determination.svg/440px-Drosophila_XY_sex-determination.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="457" data-file-height="376" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Drosophila_XY_sex-determination.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Like humans and other mammals, the common fruit fly has an <a href="/wiki/XY_sex-determination_system" title="XY sex-determination system">XY sex-determination system</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>In genetic sex-determination systems, an organism\'s sex is determined by the genome it inherits. Genetic sex-determination usually depends on asymmetrically inherited sex chromosomes which carry genetic features that influence <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">development</a>; sex may be determined either by the presence of a sex chromosome or by how many the organism has. Genetic sex-determination, because it is determined by chromosome assortment, usually results in a 1:1 ratio of male and female offspring.\n</p><p>Humans and other <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> have an <a href="/wiki/XY_sex-determination_system" title="XY sex-determination system">XY sex-determination system</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y chromosome</a> carries factors responsible for triggering male development. The "default sex," in the absence of a Y chromosome, is female-like. Thus, XX mammals are female and XY are male. In humans, biological sex is determined by five factors present at birth: the presence or absence of a Y chromosome (which alone determines the individual\'s <i>genetic sex</i>), the type of <a href="/wiki/Gonad" title="Gonad">gonads</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sex_hormone" class="mw-redirect" title="Sex hormone">sex hormones</a>, the internal reproductive anatomy (such as the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> in females), and the external genitalia.<sup id="cite_ref-KnoxSchacht2011_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KnoxSchacht2011-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>XY sex determination is found in other organisms, including the <a href="/wiki/Common_fruit_fly" class="mw-redirect" title="Common fruit fly">common fruit fly</a> and some plants.<sup id="cite_ref-dellaporta_1993_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dellaporta_1993-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In some cases, including in the fruit fly, it is the number of X chromosomes that determines sex rather than the presence of a Y chromosome (see below).\n</p><p>In birds, which have a <a href="/wiki/ZW_sex-determination_system" title="ZW sex-determination system">ZW sex-determination system</a>, the opposite is true: the W chromosome carries factors responsible for female development, and default development is male.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In this case ZZ individuals are male and ZW are female. The majority of butterflies and moths also have a ZW sex-determination system. In both XY and ZW sex determination systems, the sex chromosome carrying the critical factors is often significantly smaller, carrying little more than the genes necessary for triggering the development of a given sex.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many insects use a sex determination system based on the number of sex chromosomes. This is called <a href="/wiki/X0_sex-determination_system" title="X0 sex-determination system">X0 sex-determination</a>\xe2\x80\x94the 0 indicates the absence of the sex chromosome. All other chromosomes in these organisms are diploid, but organisms may inherit one or two X chromosomes. In <a href="/wiki/Field_cricket" class="mw-redirect" title="Field cricket">field crickets</a>, for example, insects with a single X chromosome develop as male, while those with two develop as female.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In the nematode <i><a href="/wiki/Caenorhabditis_elegans" title="Caenorhabditis elegans">C. elegans</a></i> most worms are self-fertilizing XX hermaphrodites, but occasionally abnormalities in chromosome inheritance regularly give rise to individuals with only one X chromosome\xe2\x80\x94these X0 individuals are fertile males (and half their offspring are male).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other insects, including <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>, use a <a href="/wiki/Haplodiploidy" title="Haplodiploidy">haplodiploid sex-determination system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In this case, diploid individuals are generally female, and haploid individuals (which develop from unfertilized eggs) are male. This sex-determination system results in highly biased <a href="/wiki/Sex_ratio" title="Sex ratio">sex ratios</a>, as the sex of offspring is determined by fertilization rather than the assortment of chromosomes during meiosis.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nongenetic">Nongenetic</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ocellaris_clownfish.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Ocellaris_clownfish.JPG/220px-Ocellaris_clownfish.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Ocellaris_clownfish.JPG/330px-Ocellaris_clownfish.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Ocellaris_clownfish.JPG/440px-Ocellaris_clownfish.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ocellaris_clownfish.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Clownfish" class="mw-redirect" title="Clownfish">Clownfishes</a> are initially male; the largest fish in a group becomes female</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_sex_determination" title="Environmental sex determination">Environmental sex determination</a></div>\n<p>For many species, sex is not determined by inherited traits, but instead by environmental factors experienced during development or later in life. Many reptiles have <a href="/wiki/Temperature-dependent_sex_determination" title="Temperature-dependent sex determination">temperature-dependent sex determination</a>: the temperature embryos experience during their development determines the sex of the organism. In some turtles, for example, males are produced at lower incubation temperatures than females; this difference in critical temperatures can be as little as 1\xe2\x80\x932&#160;\xc2\xb0C.\n</p><p>Many fish change sex over the course of their lifespan, a phenomenon called <a href="/wiki/Sequential_hermaphroditism" title="Sequential hermaphroditism">sequential hermaphroditism</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Clownfish" class="mw-redirect" title="Clownfish">clownfish</a>, smaller fish are male, and the dominant and largest fish in a group becomes female. In many <a href="/wiki/Wrasse" title="Wrasse">wrasses</a> the opposite is true\xe2\x80\x94most fish are initially female and become male when they reach a certain size. Sequential hermaphrodites may produce both types of gametes over the course of their lifetime, but at any given point they are either female or male.\n</p><p>In some <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a> the default sex is hermaphrodite, but ferns which grow in soil that has previously supported hermaphrodites are influenced by residual hormones to instead develop as male.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_dimorphism">Sexual dimorphism</span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">Sexual dimorphism</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Male_and_female_pheasant.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Male_and_female_pheasant.jpg/220px-Male_and_female_pheasant.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Male_and_female_pheasant.jpg/330px-Male_and_female_pheasant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Male_and_female_pheasant.jpg/440px-Male_and_female_pheasant.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Male_and_female_pheasant.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Common_pheasant" title="Common pheasant">Common pheasants</a> are <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a> in both size and appearance.</div></div></div>\n<p>Many animals and some plants have differences between the male and female sexes in size and appearance, a phenomenon called <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexual dimorphism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sex_differences_in_humans" title="Sex differences in humans">Sex differences in humans</a> include, generally, a larger size and more body hair in men; women have breasts, wider hips, and a higher body fat percentage. In other species, the differences may be more extreme, such as differences in coloration or bodyweight.\n</p><p>Sexual dimorphisms in animals are often associated with <a href="/wiki/Sexual_selection" title="Sexual selection">sexual selection</a>\xe2\x80\x94the competition between individuals of one sex to mate with the opposite sex.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Antlers in male deer, for example, are used in combat between males to win reproductive access to female deer. In many cases the male of a species is larger than the female. Mammal species with extreme sexual size dimorphism tend to have highly <a href="/wiki/Polygyny" title="Polygyny">polygynous</a> mating systems\xe2\x80\x94presumably due to selection for success in <a href="/wiki/Intraspecific_competition" title="Intraspecific competition">competition</a> with other males\xe2\x80\x94such as the <a href="/wiki/Elephant_seal" title="Elephant seal">elephant seals</a>. Other examples demonstrate that it is the preference of females that drive sexual dimorphism, such as in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Stalk-eyed_fly" title="Stalk-eyed fly">stalk-eyed fly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other animals, including most insects and many fish, have larger females. This may be associated with the cost of producing egg cells, which requires more nutrition than producing sperm\xe2\x80\x94larger females are able to produce more eggs.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> For example, female <a href="/wiki/Southern_black_widow" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern black widow">southern black widow</a> spiders are typically twice as long as the males.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Occasionally this dimorphism is extreme, with males reduced to living as <a href="/wiki/Parasite" class="mw-redirect" title="Parasite">parasites</a> dependent on the female, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Anglerfish" title="Anglerfish">anglerfish</a>. Some plant species also exhibit dimorphism in which the females are significantly larger than the males, such as in the moss <i><a href="/wiki/Dicranum" title="Dicranum">Dicranum</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and the liverwort <i><a href="/wiki/Sphaerocarpos" title="Sphaerocarpos">Sphaerocarpos</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schuster_1984_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_1984-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> There is some evidence that, in these genera, the dimorphism may be tied to a sex chromosome,<sup id="cite_ref-Schuster_1984_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schuster_1984-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Crum_&amp;_Anderson_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crum_&amp;_Anderson-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> or to chemical signalling from females.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In birds, males often have a more <a href="/wiki/Animal_coloration" title="Animal coloration">colourful</a> appearance and may have features (like the long tail of male peacocks) that would seem to put the organism at a disadvantage (e.g. bright colors would seem to make a bird more visible to predators). One proposed explanation for this is the <a href="/wiki/Handicap_principle" title="Handicap principle">handicap principle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> This hypothesis says that, by demonstrating he can survive with such handicaps, the male is advertising his <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">genetic fitness</a> to females\xe2\x80\x94traits that will benefit daughters as well, who will not be encumbered with such handicaps.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sex_and_gender_distinction" title="Sex and gender distinction">Sex and gender distinction</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Sex_assignment" title="Sex assignment">Sex assignment</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-Stevenson-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-Stevenson_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Angus Stevenson, Maurice Waite (2011). <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=4XycAQAAQBAJ&amp;pg=PA1320"><i>Concise Oxford English Dictionary: Book &amp; CD-ROM Set</i></a>. <a href="/wiki/OUP_Oxford" class="mw-redirect" title="OUP Oxford">OUP Oxford</a>. p.&#160;1302. <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Book_Number" title="International Standard Book Number">ISBN</a>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/978-0-19-960110-3" title="Special:BookSources/978-0-19-960110-3"><bdi>978-0-19-960110-3</bdi></a><span class="reference-accessdate">.
